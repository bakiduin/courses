Classification of Embedded System

Embedded systems can be classified based on various factors, including performance, functionality, complexity, and triggering mechanism. Here are some common classifications


Based on Performance and Functional Requirements

Real-Time Embedded Systems:

  • These systems must produce outputs within strict time constraints.

    They are further classified into:

    • Hard Real-Time Systems:

      Require absolute deadlines for task completion. Failure to meet deadlines can lead to catastrophic consequences.

    • Soft Real-Time Systems:

      Less stringent timing requirements. Missing deadlines can degrade performance but won't cause catastrophic failure.

Networked Embedded Systems:

  • These systems interact with other systems or networks.

  • They can be wired or wireless.

Mobile Embedded Systems:

  • These systems are portable and battery-powered.

  • Examples include smartphones, tablets, and wearables.

Standalone Embedded Systems:

  • These systems operate independently without external connections.

  • Examples include digital watches and simple remote controls.


Based on Generation:

First Generation:

  • Built around 8-bit microprocessors and microcontrollers.

  • Simple hardware and firmware.

  • Examples: Digital telephone keypads.

Second Generation:

  • Built around 16-bit microprocessors and 8-bit microcontrollers.

  • More complex and powerful than first-generation systems.

  • Examples: Early mobile phones.

Third Generation:

  • Built around 32-bit microprocessors and 16-bit microcontrollers.

  • Introduced concepts like Digital Signal Processors (DSPs) and Application-Specific Integrated Circuits (ASICs).

  • Examples: Advanced mobile phones, digital cameras.

Fourth Generation:

  • Built around powerful microprocessors and microcontrollers with advanced features.

  • Emphasis on low power consumption, high performance, and networking capabilities

  • Examples: Smart home devices, autonomous vehicles.


Based on Triggering Mechanism:

Event-Triggered Systems:

  • Respond to specific events or interrupts.

  • Examples: Alarm systems, security cameras.

Time-Triggered Systems:

  • Execute tasks at predefined time intervals.

  • Execute tasks at predefined time intervals.


Based on Complexity:

Small-Scale Embedded Systems:

  • Simple systems with limited functionality.

  • Examples: Remote controls, digital clocks.

Medium-Scale Embedded Systems:

  • More complex systems with advanced features.

  • Examples: Digital cameras, printers.

Sophisticated Embedded Systems:

  • Highly complex systems with advanced processing capabilities.

  • Examples: Autonomous vehicles, medical devices.


Understanding these classifications helps in designing, developing, and deploying embedded systems for various applications.
